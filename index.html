About This Project

Walmart Retail Store Analytics — Exploratory Data Analysis

This dashboard presents an exploratory data analysis (EDA) of Walmart's retail operations across 45 stores over a ~3.5-year period (February 2010 – July 2013). The dataset comprises two sources: features.csv, which captures weekly economic and promotional indicators such as fuel prices, CPI, unemployment rates, temperature, and markdown campaigns, and stores.csv, which describes each store's type and physical size.

The primary objective is to understand how store characteristics (type, size) interact with external economic conditions (inflation, unemployment, fuel costs) and internal promotional levers (markdown campaigns, holiday flags) to shape the retail environment. By surfacing these patterns visually, we aim to identify actionable insights around store operations, promotional planning, and macroeconomic sensitivity — laying the groundwork for downstream predictive modeling such as weekly sales forecasting.

📈 CPI & unemployment trend (quarterly, 2010–2013)
dual axis
CPI rose steadily while unemployment declined.
CPI index (left axis) Unemployment % (right axis)
📌 Finding

The dual-axis chart reveals a clear macroeconomic recovery narrative over the 2010–2013 period. CPI rose steadily from 167.9 to 178.0 (+6.0%), indicating modest but consistent consumer-price inflation. Simultaneously, the unemployment rate fell from a peak of 8.62% in early 2010 to 6.72% by mid-2013 — a meaningful 1.9 percentage-point improvement. The inverse relationship between these two indicators is consistent with a post-recession recovery: as the labour market tightened, purchasing power improved. For retail, this backdrop suggests gradually improving consumer confidence, which may have supported volume growth over the analysis window.

🌡️ Seasonal temperature & fuel price pattern
bi-monthly sample
Temperature shows seasonal cycles. Fuel price trended upward 2010–2012.
Temperature (°F, left axis) Fuel price $/gal (right axis)
📌 Finding

Temperature exhibits a pronounced and predictable seasonal cycle, oscillating between ~36–40°F in winter months (Feb) and ~75°F in summer (Jun), with no discernible year-over-year shift — confirming it as a reliable seasonal control variable. Fuel prices tell a different story: they surged sharply from ~$2.69/gallon in early 2010 to a peak of ~$3.85 by late 2012 — a 43% increase — before stabilising in 2013 around $3.58–$3.62. This structural fuel price rise over the period likely exerted sustained upward pressure on both operational costs and consumer budgets, making it a meaningful covariate in any retail sales model. Notably, fuel peaks tend to coincide with summer months, amplifying seasonal cost pressures simultaneously.
